The Sport of Archery

Archery began as a tool for survival then warfare and sport combined, but this changed as firearms became the main ranged weapon used in war, causing archery to become fully an activity only used for fun. This shift finished many centuries ago in the year 1583, where in Finsbury, England, many believed the first large scale archery competition, for fun, happened.

True Sport Beginnings | (16th-19th Century)

With gunpowder based weapons, the bow and arrow became obsolete as a primary weapon of war and this led to a big change. Archery changed from a military necessity to a fun activity, with Archery clubs and societies beginning to form in England, which held annual tournaments. The first large scale competition being in 1583 in Finsbury, England. In the 19th century it changed again to become a more formalized sport and the first Grand National Archery Society meeting was held in York in 1844, and the "York Round" (many shots at different distances) became a standardized format.

Olympics Introduction & Troubles | (1900-1920)

Archery was part of the very first modern Olympic Games in 1900. It also appeared in the 1904, 1908, and 1920 Games. However, after 1920, the sport was removed from the Olympics. The reason was a lack of standardized rules; each country used its own scoring and equipment rules. This made the competition unfair and difficult to manage on a global scale, leading to a long absence from the Olympics.

International Standardisation | (1931 - 1972)

Archery returned to the Olympic Games after a long absence, thanks to the creation of the "Fédération Internationale de Tir à l'Arc" (FITA) in 1931, now known as "World Archery". This global governing body was formed with one main goal: to create a single set of standardized rules for all international competitions. By unifying the scoring, equipment, and competition formats, FITA made the sport fair and consistent for all nations. This hard work paid off, and archery was permanently reintroduced into the Olympic program at the 1972 Munich Games, starting its modern era as a major international sport.

Modern Day | (1972-Present)

Since archery's return to the Olympics in 1972, the sport has entered a new age of technological advancement and global competition. Recurve bows, the only bow type allowed in the Olympics, have been refined with modern materials like carbon fiber and aluminum, making them more consistent and durable. Additionally, the modern sport has seen the rise of the compound bow, which, with its system of pulleys and cables, has made archery more accessible and accurate for recreational shooters and hunters, as well as in other competitive formats like 3D and field archery.

Conclusion

Through its long and dynamic history, archery has evolved from a vital skill for hunting and war into a globally recognized sport. After facing challenges with a lack of standardized rules, the sport earned its permanent place in the Olympics in 1972. Today, archery continues to connect ancient traditions of focus and discipline with the advancements of modern technology, creating a sport that is both timeless and innovative.
